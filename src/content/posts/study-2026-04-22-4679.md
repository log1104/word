# Bible Study: Genesis 2

**You:** Study Genesis 2:2... In paritcular the word day, that day has not ended

In [Genesis 2:2](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+2%3A2), the text records the transition from the activity of creation to the rest of the Creator:

> *"By the seventh day God had finished the work He had been doing; so on the seventh day He rested from all His work."* (BSB)

Your observation that the "seventh day" has not ended is a profound insight shared by many careful students of Scripture and supported by the internal structure of the Genesis narrative and later New Testament commentary.

### The Missing Refrain
In Genesis 1, every day of creation follows a specific rhythmic pattern: *"And there was evening, and there was morning—the [first, second, etc.] day."* 

*   [Genesis 1:5](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+1%3A5) (Day 1)
*   [Genesis 1:8](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+1%3A8) (Day 2)
*   [Genesis 1:13](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+1%3A13) (Day 3)
*   [Genesis 1:19](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+1%3A19) (Day 4)
*   [Genesis 1:23](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+1%3A23) (Day 5)
*   [Genesis 1:31](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+1%3A31) (Day 6)

However, when we reach the seventh day in [Genesis 2:2-3](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+2%3A2-3), the phrase *"there was evening and there was morning"* is notably absent. In the logic of the Hebrew narrative, this suggests that while the first six days were bounded periods of creative activity, the seventh day—the day of God’s rest—is a state or an epoch that does not have a recorded "evening." 

### The Meaning of "Day"
The word used for "day" is the Hebrew **yom** ([Strong's H3117](https://biblehub.com/strongs/hebrew/3117.htm)). While *yom* often refers to a literal 24-hour period, it is also used throughout Scripture to denote a specific season, a time of judgment, or an indefinite period of time (such as "the day of the Lord"). 

In the context of [Genesis 2:2](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+2%3A2), God's "rest" (**shabat**, [Strong's H7673](https://biblehub.com/strongs/hebrew/7673.htm)) does not mean He became inactive or tired. Rather, it signifies that His creative work was *complete*. Because that work remains finished, the "day" of that finished work continues.

### Scriptural Confirmation: Hebrews 4
The strongest biblical evidence for your view that the seventh day has not ended comes from [Hebrews 4:1–11](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Hebrews+4%3A1-11). The author of Hebrews argues that God’s rest began at the foundation of the world and is still available for people to enter today.

*   **[Hebrews 4:3](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Hebrews+4%3A3):** *"And yet His works have been finished since the creation of the world."*
*   **[Hebrews 4:9-10](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Hebrews+4%3A9-10):** *"There remains, then, a Sabbath rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God’s rest also rests from their own work, just as God did from His."*

If the seventh day were merely a 24-hour period that concluded before the Fall, the author of Hebrews could not invite readers thousands of years later to enter into that same rest. This suggests that the "Seventh Day" is the current era of God’s finished creation, which will eventually find its ultimate fulfillment in the New Creation.

### Why This Matters
Understanding the seventh day as an ongoing reality changes how we view our relationship with God:

1.  **Rest is a State, Not Just a Day:** It teaches us that "Sabbath" is not just about a calendar date, but about dwelling in the finished work of God.
2.  **Grace over Effort:** Just as God rested because the work was done, we are invited to rest in the finished work of Christ ([John 19:30](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=John+19%3A30)). 
3.  **The Goal of History:** It reminds us that history is moving toward a permanent "rest" where the "evening" of sin and death is finally removed ([Revelation 21:25](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Revelation+21%3A25)).

In summary, the text of [Genesis 2:2](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+2%3A2) purposefully leaves the seventh day "open-ended" to signal that God's rest is a permanent reality that He invites His creation to share.

**You:** Christ said he is still working as the Father is still working.

You have pinpointed one of the most important tensions in the study of Scripture: How can God be "at rest" while Jesus claims He is "still working"?

The answer lies in the **nature** of the work being discussed. When we compare [Genesis 2:2](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+2%3A2) with [John 5:17](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=John+5%3A17), we see that God has ceased one type of work while continuing another.

### 1. The Work of Creation vs. The Work of Providence
In [Genesis 2:2](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+2%3A2), the word for "rested" is **shabat** ([Strong's H7673](https://biblehub.com/strongs/hebrew/7673.htm)), which literally means "to cease" or "to stop." 

The text is specific about what God stopped: *"He rested from all His work."* In context, this refers to the work of **Genesis 1**—the bringing of the physical universe into existence from nothing. That specific "work" is finished. God is no longer creating new galaxies, new species, or new physical laws. In this sense, the "Seventh Day" continues because the creation is complete.

### 2. Jesus’ Defense in John 5:17
In [John 5](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=John+5), Jesus heals a man on the Sabbath. When the religious leaders confront Him for "working" on the day of rest, Jesus responds:

> *"My Father is always at His work to this very day, and I too am working."* ([John 5:17](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=John+5%3A17))

The Greek word for "working" here is **ergazomai** ([Strong's G2038](https://biblehub.com/strongs/greek/2038.htm)). Jesus is making a profound theological point: While God "ceased" from the work of **Creation**, He never "ceased" from the work of **Providence** (sustaining the world) and **Redemption** (saving the world).

Even on the Sabbath, God:
*   Causes the sun to rise and the rain to fall ([Matthew 5:45](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Matthew+5%3A45)).
*   Sustains all things by the word of His power ([Hebrews 1:3](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Hebrews+1%3A3)).
*   Shows mercy and brings healing to the broken.

Jesus was essentially saying, "If my Father stopped 'working' to sustain life on the Sabbath, the universe would collapse. Since He continues to do good on the Sabbath, I, His Son, am doing the same."

### 3. Rest and Activity Coexisting
Biblically, God’s "rest" is not a state of sleep or inactivity; it is a state of **enthronement**. In the Ancient Near East, a deity "rested" in a temple once it was completed so that he could rule from it. 

When God "rested" on the seventh day, He took His seat as the King of the completed creation. From that position of "rest" (completion), He "works" to govern and redeem His people. 

*   **Genesis 2:2** describes the **Finished Product** (Creation is done).
*   **John 5:17** describes the **Ongoing Care** (Redemption is active).

### Summary
The "Seventh Day" remains open in the sense that God has not returned to the "work" of creating new worlds. We are living in the era of His completed creation. However, because that creation fell into sin ([Genesis 3](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Genesis+3)), God began a *new* work—the work of salvation—which Jesus was carrying out.

As [Colossians 1:17](https://www.openbible.info/labs/cross-references/search?q=Colossians+1%3A17) says of Christ: *"He is before all things, and in Him all things hold together."* He is "at rest" in His finished creative work, yet "working" every moment to hold your very life together.
